## 1) What have I been doing this week? What skills did I need?

In Week 4, the focus remained on the membership homepage redesign of the European Heat Pump Association.

Following the prototype development in Week 3, the proposed redesign was shared internally so colleagues and managers could review the structure and messaging. Because the membership page is an important entry point for potential members, the design needs to be validated before moving forward to implementation.

During this week, we were awaiting feedback from senior colleagues and management regarding the prototype and the proposed structural improvements.

While waiting for this input, a significant part of the week was dedicated to reviewing, testing, and refining the structure of the homepage. Together with the team, we continued exploring whether the proposed structure clearly communicates the value of membership and effectively guides visitors toward becoming members.

To test different structural ideas quickly, we first experimented with sketching alternative layouts on paper. These quick sketches allowed us to reorganize sections, test different hierarchies, and discuss potential user flows without immediately committing to a digital design.

Some of these discussions also involved colleagues from outside our department, which allowed us to gather fresh perspectives from people who were less familiar with the project. Their reactions helped us evaluate whether the page structure was intuitive and whether the value proposition was immediately understandable.

After discussing and testing ideas through sketches, we translated the most promising structures into digital prototype drafts on the computer. This helped us visualize how the reorganized sections would appear on the actual page and allowed us to refine the hierarchy, layout, and calls-to-action more precisely.

Throughout this process, we focused on:

- Improving the clarity of the value proposition
- Ensuring that membership benefits are visible and easy to understand
- Strengthening the hierarchy and logical flow of the homepage
- Reviewing the placement and visibility of calls-to-action guiding visitors toward membership

Another important objective of the redesign was to reduce reliance on the static membership benefits PDF by integrating the most relevant information directly into the homepage in a clearer and more engaging way.

During these discussions, we also tried to better understand the perspective of potential members visiting the page. From a user experience and psychological perspective, we considered how the page could encourage visitors to feel interested and confident about becoming members.

Because the organization regularly hosts events and maintains an active presence on social media, many people who reach the membership page may already be familiar with the organization and its activities. With this in mind, we discussed how the page structure could better support users who already have some awareness of the organization but may still feel uncertain about taking the next step toward membership.

A large part of the redesign therefore focused on making the process of becoming a member feel more intuitive, approachable, and clearly explained. We explored ways to simplify the page structure so that visitors can quickly understand the benefits of joining and feel comfortable exploring membership options.

At the same time, we also discussed how the interface could feel more engaging and visually dynamic, bringing more life to the page while still respecting the organization's branding guidelines.

Throughout the week, I actively participated in brainstorming sessions and planning discussions regarding the page structure. I suggested several adjustments related to section ordering, clarity of messaging, and the presentation of membership benefits. Some of these suggestions were incorporated into the evolving prototype.

In addition, I helped organize and document the feedback gathered during discussions, writing structured notes summarizing key comments and proposed improvements. This helped ensure that the most relevant insights would be incorporated into the next revision of the prototype.

Overall, my work this week involved participating in brainstorming sessions, testing ideas through sketches, reviewing structural improvements, documenting feedback, and helping refine the prototype design before final validation.

This week required:

- UX evaluation and analytical thinking
- Collaborative brainstorming and discussion
- Clear communication of design ideas
- Structured documentation of feedback
- Understanding how stakeholder validation influences design decisions

The main objective was to ensure that the membership homepage prototype was ready for final feedback and eventual implementation.

## 3) What did I learn this week? In what way?

This week showed me how iterative testing and collaboration can significantly improve UX decisions.

Even simple techniques such as sketching layouts on paper and discussing them with colleagues can quickly reveal whether a structure works well or needs improvement.

I learned that:

- Quick sketches can help test ideas before building digital prototypes
- Feedback from colleagues outside the immediate project team can provide valuable perspectives
- Understanding the user's mindset is essential when designing pages intended to encourage action
- Implementation should only begin once the structure and messaging are clearly validated
