## 1) What have I been doing this week? What skills did I need?

In Week 6, I started implementing the membership homepage in WordPress for the first time, based on the validated structure and finalized content from Week 5.

This marked a major shift in my role. Up until now, I had mainly been involved in analysis, structuring, and design discussions. This week, I was responsible for actually building the page myself.

For the first time, I was working on a real website where the changes I made would directly impact the final result. This made the experience both exciting and challenging.

Although WordPress uses familiar concepts such as containers, grids, and headers, I quickly realized that applying them in practice was very different from understanding them theoretically. The week involved a steep learning curve.

A large part of my time was spent working on the header section and the “Why Join EHPA” section. These components required multiple iterations to get right.

I often reached a point where the design looked correct visually, but did not behave properly across different screen sizes. For example, I initially used a grid layout where a flexbox approach would have been more appropriate. While it looked fine at first, it caused issues with responsiveness, which meant I had to rebuild parts of the layout.

This trial-and-error process happened several times throughout the week.

Each time something did not work as expected, I received feedback and guidance from the team. They explained not only how to fix the issue, but also why a different approach was better. This helped me develop a deeper understanding of layout systems and implementation logic.

To better understand and control the layout, I worked directly within the WordPress editor, adjusting containers, layout settings, and styling options. Below are some screenshots from the implementation process while working on the header:

## 3) What did I learn this week? In what way?

This week was one of the most intense learning experiences so far.

I learned that:

- Implementing a design is very different from designing it
- Layout decisions (such as grid vs flexbox) directly impact responsiveness
- Trial and error is a normal and necessary part of development
- Feedback is essential to improving technical understanding
- CMS environments require adapting design ideas to technical constraints

I also learned how important it is to understand not just *what* works, but *why* it works.
