## 1) What have I been doing this week? What skills did I need?

In Week 12, I focused on implementing the final feedback adjustments for the membership homepage and refining the page to ensure a more polished and consistent user experience.

Following the feedback received at the end of the previous week, I worked on improving several aspects of the implementation. This included refining responsiveness for larger screens, improving visual consistency across sections, and making smaller UI adjustments throughout the page.

Some of the refinements included:

- Improving responsiveness on larger desktop displays  
- Replacing abbreviations with the full "European Heat Pump Association" name for better clarity  
- Ensuring button formatting remained consistent across the page  
- Adjusting logo sizes and spacing for better visual balance  
- Refining alignment and section consistency  

A major topic this week was the final section involving the member map.

After reviewing the current implementation and discussing it internally, we collectively decided that the embedded map approach was not the right solution for the project. While the original concept worked technically, it did not fully align with the overall user experience, visual direction, and integration quality of the redesigned page.

As a result, we decided to move away from the embedded map and replace it with a different map solution that better fits the design and usability goals of the project.

This decision required stepping back and reevaluating how the member locations should be presented in a clearer, more visually integrated, and more user-friendly way.

Throughout the week, we discussed:

- Alternative ways to display membership locations  
- How the map could feel more integrated into the page design  
- Improving clarity and interaction  
- Creating a solution that feels lighter and more modern

In addition to the map redesign decision, we also reviewed the gallery section of the page.

After testing and evaluating the current implementation, we felt that the gallery looked visually awkward and did not integrate naturally with the rest of the page. Because of this, we decided to redesign the gallery approach as well and move toward a slightly different implementation that would feel cleaner and more aligned with the rest of the interface.

This required reconsidering:

- The layout and presentation of images  
- The spacing and visual rhythm of the section  
- How the gallery integrates into the user journey  
- Creating a more balanced and modern appearance  

Another significant design decision this week was the removal of the yellow bottom section from the page.

After reviewing the overall flow and hierarchy of the interface, we concluded that this section did not add enough value to justify its presence and visually disrupted the consistency of the final page. Removing it helped simplify the interface and improve the overall visual balance and clarity of the experience.

In parallel to the membership page refinements, I also continued contributing to the Heat Pump Forum programme redesign.

During this week, I received feedback regarding the programme structure proposal I had previously created for the Forum page.

Part of my proposed structure for Day 1 was accepted and has already been implemented and published on the live website.

One of the main suggestions I made was to improve readability and hierarchy by restructuring how the programme content is presented. Based on my proposal, the speaker-focused layout was adjusted and more emphasis was placed on the session descriptions, making the programme easier to scan and understand for visitors.

The updated version is now live here:

https://ehpa.org/heatpumpforum/programme_schedule/

Seeing elements of my proposed structure implemented on a real production website was highly motivating and showed me that my ideas are contributing to actual published projects.

However, the proposal for Day 2 could not be implemented in the same way. The second day of the event is not directly organized by the organization itself, meaning they legally cannot market it as their own official “Day 2” programme.

Despite this limitation, the team still appreciated the direction of my proposal and asked me to think about a creative solution for how the main page could still reference Day 2 without incorrectly presenting ownership or taking direct credit for organizing it.

This introduced an additional UX and communication challenge, where legal and branding considerations also influence design and content decisions.

Overall, this week focused less on building entirely new sections and more on refinement, UX evaluation, and improving the quality and consistency of the final experience.

This week required:

- Attention to detail  
- Responsive design adjustments  
- UX evaluation and problem-solving  
- Collaboration and feedback integration  
- HTML, CSS, and WordPress refinement  
- UX communication thinking

## 3) What did I learn this week? In what way?

This week reinforced how important refinement and evaluation are during the final stages of a project.

I learned that:

- Final polishing and consistency significantly improve perceived quality  
- Responsiveness must also be tested on larger screens, not only mobile devices  
- Sometimes replacing a solution is more effective than continuing to refine an unsuitable one  
- UX decisions should prioritize integration and user clarity over technical convenience  
- Simplifying a page can improve overall usability and visual balance  
- Legal and communication considerations can directly influence UX decisions  
- Small visual inconsistencies can impact the overall professionalism of a page  

I also realized that being willing to rethink and replace parts of a project is an important part of professional UX and development work.
